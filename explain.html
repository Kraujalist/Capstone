How Heat Vulnerability Works

Understanding the science behind neighborhood heat risk in the Netherlands

The Big Picture

Heat waves have become more extreme. But here's what most climate apps don't tell you: the danger isn't evenly distributed.

Your neighbor might be fine during a heat wave while another neighborhood just blocks away faces a health crisis. Why? Because heat vulnerability depends on who lives there and what condition their homes are in—not just how hot it gets.

This tool brings together three types of data to predict which neighborhoods are most at risk: climate conditions, demographic information, and actual health outcomes. The result? A clear picture of where Dutch communities need help.

Where the Data Comes From

We combined three major Dutch government datasets:

Dataset Source Tells Us
Climate Data KlimaatAtlas 2024 How hot it actually feels (perceived temp) in each neighborhood
Who Lives Where CBS (Statistics Netherlands) Age, income levels, housing types, and population sizes
Real Health Data GGD (Municipal Health Services) How people actually report their health and stress levels

Analysis includes 11,554 neighborhoods across the Netherlands with complete data

How We Built the Model

We used Linear Regression—think of it as finding the strongest patterns in the data. We trained it on real neighborhoods, watching which factors predict high health vulnerability best.

The beauty of this approach? It's transparent. You can see exactly how much each factor matters.

The Formula (If You're Curious)

Vulnerability = -1332.3467 + 4.17 × Temperature + 3.44 × Elderly% + 2.98 × Low-Income% + 2.89 × Children% + 0.66 × OldBuildings% + 0.66 × StressLevel% +

What This Means

We expanded the original 4-factor model to include 6 key factors. The model now explains 72% of the variance in health vulnerability (R² = 0.7220)—a significant improvement. Notably, the elderly population emerged as more important than low income, and we added three critical new factors: children (young populations are vulnerable too), old buildings (poor insulation/cooling), and stress (mental health directly impacts heat resilience).

A Real Example

An Amsterdam neighborhood (7-factor model):
  • Summer temperature feels like 44°C → +4.17 × 44 = +183.5
  • 18% seniors (65+) → +3.44 × 18 = +61.9
  • 22% low-income → +2.98 × 22 = +65.6
  • 8% children (0-14) → +2.89 × 8 = +23.1
  • 78% buildings pre-2010 → +0.66 × 78 = +51.5
  • 310 stress index (raw scale) → +0.66 × 310 = +204.6

Calculation: -1268.37 + 183.5 + 61.9 + 65.6 + 23.1 + 51.5 + 204.6 + 39.7 = -638.2 (raw score)

Normalized Result: ~42/100 (moderate risk)—higher than the 4-factor model because we now account for building age and stress impacts.

📈 What Drives Heat Vulnerability?

The enhanced model identified seven key factors. Here they are, ranked by impact:

1️⃣ Temperature (+4.17)

The biggest factor. Higher perceived temperature = higher risk. It's what you feel, accounting for wind, humidity, and sun radiation—not just the thermometer reading. Ranges from 35°C to 50°C across the Netherlands.

2️⃣ Elderly Population (+3.44)

The age factor. Seniors are more physically vulnerable during heat waves. They take medications that affect heat tolerance, have more health conditions, and often live alone without support. This emerged as MORE important than income in the expanded model.

3️⃣ Low Income (+2.98)

The inequality factor. Lower-income residents can't afford air conditioning, often live in older buildings, and have less access to cooling centers and health resources. Every 10% more low-income residents = vulnerability jump of ~30 points.

4️⃣ Children Population (+2.89)

The young vulnerability factor (NEW). Young children (0-14) are heat-vulnerable populations too. They have immature thermoregulation, can't self-care during heat stress, and depend on caregivers. This was a critical gap in the original model.

5️⃣ Old Buildings (+0.66)

The building quality factor (NEW). Buildings constructed before 2010 typically have poor insulation, no air conditioning, and modern cooling infrastructure. Neighborhoods with more old buildings = higher heat risk.

6️⃣ Stress Levels (+0.66)

The mental health factor (NEW). High psychological stress directly reduces heat resilience. People under stress have weaker immune responses to heat, higher risk of heat stroke, and poor decision-making during heat waves. Actual stress data from health surveys.

7️⃣ Rental Housing (+0.61)

The housing stability factor. Renters often occupy older, poorly insulated buildings and can't independently invest in cooling solutions. It signals neighborhood conditions and residential instability.

If current emissions continue. Significantly warmer. Average vulnerability jumps ~16 points. Red zones expand, pushing more neighborhoods into crisis levels.

🌿 2050 + Holistic Policy

What if we invest in multiple interventions? Urban greening (parks, trees, roof gardens) PLUS community mental health programs to reduce stress. This combined policy reduces vulnerability by ~10-12 points compared to 2050 baseline—equivalent to delaying warming by 2-3 years. Proof that comprehensive action works better than single-factor solutions.

Big Takeaways

  • Temperature dominates, but poverty and housing quality matter enormously. An air-conditioned luxury apartment in a hot area beats a poor neighborhood in cooler climate.
  • Inequality amplifies risk. The poorest neighborhoods get hit hardest by both heat and they have fewest resources to cope.
  • Policy works. Urban greening can reduce vulnerability as much as 2-3 years of delayed warming. Cities have real power to protect their residents.
  • It's getting worse before it gets better. Even under best-case climate scenarios, heat vulnerability increases significantly by 2050.
  • Red zones matter most. A neighborhood in the 80s is in crisis. These need immediate emergency planning, cooling centers, health monitoring.

Important Caveats

  • Neighborhood averages hide variation. Within each neighborhood, pockets of extreme vulnerability exist. Block-by-block variation is much higher.
  • We assume people don't adapt. In reality, some will buy A/C, move, or build community support. Our projections are conservative.
  • Not all data is equal. 11,554 neighborhoods included; some neighborhoods lack health data and are estimated.
  • Climate projections have uncertainty. +1.5°C or +3.0°C are ranges, not certainties. Real outcomes depend on global decisions right now.
  • This is one model. Use alongside other tools and local expertise. The best decisions combine data with community knowledge.

The Machine Learning Model

Model Type: Multiple Linear Regression

We use Linear Regression because:

  • Interpretability: Easy to explain to policymakers
  • Transparency: Each factor's impact is clearly visible
  • Validated: Well-established statistical method

Training Data

  • Neighborhoods trained on: 11,554
  • Data year: 2024
  • Geographic coverage: All Dutch neighborhoods with health data

The Formula

Health Vulnerability (raw) = -1047.90 + 4.46 × PET_temperature + 3.25 × Percentage_Low_Income + 1.43 × Percentage_Elderly + 1.40 × Percentage_Rental_Housing

Example Calculation

Amsterdam Neighborhood (hypothetical):
  • PET = 44°C
  • Low-income residents = 25%
  • Elderly (65+) = 15%
  • Rental housing = 60%
Calculation:
-1047.90 + 4.46(44) + 3.25(25) + 1.43(15) + 1.40(60)
= -1047.90 + 196.24 + 81.25 + 21.45 + 84.00
= -665.0 (raw score)

After normalization to 0-100: ~35/100 (Moderate Risk)

Normalization

Raw model outputs range from -803 to -334. To make them interpretable, all scores are normalized to a 0-100 scale:

Normalized Score = 100 × (Raw Score - Minimum) / (Maximum - Minimum)

This ensures 0 = lowest risk and 100 = highest risk consistent across all scenarios.

📈 Model Features & Their Impact

Each factor contributes to the overall vulnerability score. Higher coefficients mean stronger impact.

PET Temperature (Coefficient: +4.46) HIGHEST IMPACT

What it measures: Perceived Temperature in °C - how hot it feels INCLUDING wind, humidity, and radiation
Impact: Every 1°C increase = +4.46 points vulnerability
Range: 35°C to 50°C across Netherlands
Why it matters: Direct measure of heat stress on the body

Low-Income Population (Coefficient: +3.25) 2nd HIGHEST

What it measures: Percentage of residents in bottom 40% income distribution
Impact: Every +1% low-income = +3.25 points vulnerability
Range: 7% to 95% across neighborhoods
Why it matters: Lower ability to afford air conditioning, healthcare, or cool places to escape

👴 Elderly Population (Coefficient: +1.43)

What it measures: Percentage of residents 65 years or older
Impact: Every +1% elderly = +1.43 points vulnerability
Range: 0% to 100% (some neighborhoods no elderly, others majority)
Why it matters: Elderly have reduced ability to thermoregulate; more health conditions

Rental Housing (Coefficient: +1.40)

What it measures: Percentage of rented (not owner-occupied) homes
Impact: Every +1% rental = +1.40 points vulnerability
Range: 0% to 100%
Why it matters: Renters often occupy older buildings with poor insulation; less control over renovations

Model Limitations & Assumptions

  • Cross-sectional design: Model trained on 2024 data; assumes relationships don't change dramatically
  • Neighborhood aggregation: Vulnerability masks variation within neighborhoods (e.g., poorer blocks vs. wealthy blocks)
  • Health data gaps: Only 11,554 of 14,668 neighborhoods have health data; missing areas interpolated
  • PET measurement: Based on KlimaatAtlas grid; local microclimates not captured
  • Linear model assumption: Assumes linear relationships between features and vulnerability (may not capture complex interactions)
  • Future projections: Assumes no behavioral changes (e.g., people don't necessarily move, invest in cooling, etc.)
  • Social factors unmeasured: Community cohesion, access to services, migration patterns not directly in model

Key Insights

  • Temperature is dominant: Heat stress (+4.46) is the strongest predictor, roughly 40% more impactful than income
  • Inequality matters: Low-income and rental housing together account for ~45% of model weight
  • Aging compounds risk: While elderly population affects only ~10% of total variation, it's an independent vulnerability factor
  • Urban-rural divide: Cities typically show higher rental %, while rural areas have more elderly—creating different risk profiles
  • Policy works: Urban greening reduces vulnerability by ~10 points—comparable to delaying warming by ~2-3 years
  • Existing disparities grow: High-vulnerability areas get worse with warming; low-vulnerability areas relatively stable

Technical Reference

Model Statistics

  • Training samples: 11,554 neighborhoods
  • Target variable: Inverted good health perception (0-100 scale)
  • Input features: 4 (PET, low-income %, elderly %, rental %)
  • Model type: Scikit-learn LinearRegression
  • No regularization: Raw linear model; emphasis on interpretability

Data Processing

# Load data from multiple sources health_data (GGD 2024) + CBS_data (demographics) + climate_data (KlimaatAtlas) # Filter to neighborhoods with complete data neighborhoods = health_data ∩ CBS_data ∩ climate_data # 11,554 neighborhoods # Handle missing values drop rows with NaN in key features # Scale target target = 100 - good_health_perception # invert to measure vulnerability # Normalize predictions to 0-100 normalized_score = 100 * (raw_prediction - min) / (max - min) # Generate future scenarios climate_2050 = features + [PET += 1.5] climate_2075 = features + [PET += 3.0] policy_2050 = climate_2050 + [rental % -= 15]
