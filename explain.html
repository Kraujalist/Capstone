01

The Big Picture

Heat waves are intensifying. But most climate tools miss the real story: danger isn't evenly distributed.

Your neighbor might be fine while a neighborhood blocks away faces a health crisis. The difference isn't just how hot it gets—it's who lives there and what condition their homes are in.

This tool merges three types of Dutch government data—climate conditions, demographics, and real health outcomes—to reveal where communities are most at risk. Not a rough estimate: a neighborhood-level portrait of vulnerability across all 11,554 neighborhoods in the Netherlands.

03

The Model

We use Multiple Linear Regression—trained on real neighborhoods to find which factors best predict health vulnerability. The approach is intentionally transparent: every factor's contribution is visible and auditable.

The refined 6-factor model explains the majority of variance in health vulnerability (trained on 11,554 neighborhoods).

The Equation

Vulnerability = −1332.347 + 4.32 × Temperature + 4.05 × Elderly % + 3.40 × Low-Income % + 2.99 × Children % + 0.93 × Stress Level % + 0.61 × Old Buildings %

Raw scores are normalized to a 0–100 scale: 0 = lowest risk, 100 = highest risk.

What Drives Vulnerability

Six core factors, ranked by their model coefficient (impact per unit increase):

01 · Highest impact
Temperature
+4.32 per °C
Perceived temperature (PET) accounts for wind, humidity, and solar radiation. It remains the strongest predictor of neighborhood-level heat distress.
02
Elderly Population
+4.05 per %
Seniors face reduced thermoregulation and greater social isolation. This factor's impact has significantly increased in the refined model.
03
Low Income
+3.40 per %
Financial barriers to air conditioning, energy-efficient housing, and private healthcare. A critical amplifier of physiological heat risk.
04
Children
+2.99 per %
Young children (0–14) are highly sensitive to heat due to immature physiological responses and reliance on adult care for hydration/cooling.
05
Stress Levels
+0.93 per %
High psychological stress (GGD survey data) correlates with lower resilience and higher reported health complications during extreme heat.
06
Old Buildings
+0.61 per %
Structures built before 2010 often suffer from poor thermal mass and lack modern insulation, leading to dangerous indoor heat retention.

07

Key Takeaways

🌡

Temperature dominates, but doesn't determine everything

An air-conditioned apartment in a hot area can beat a poor neighbourhood in a cooler one. Climate is the trigger; social conditions are the amplifier.

⚖️

Inequality amplifies risk

The poorest neighbourhoods are hit hardest and have the fewest resources to cope. Disparity doesn't just co-exist with heat risk—it multiplies it.

🌿

Policy genuinely works

Urban greening reduces vulnerability as much as 2–3 years of delayed warming. Cities have real power to protect their residents right now.

📈

It gets worse before 2050

Even under the most optimistic climate scenario, average heat vulnerability increases significantly by mid-century.

🔴

Red zones are crises, not warnings

Neighborhoods scoring 80+ need emergency-level planning: cooling centres, health monitoring, proactive outreach—not just awareness.

🏗

Building age is underrated

Old housing stock quietly traps heat and lacks cooling infrastructure. Renovation programmes may matter as much as green space investment.

Using the Dashboard

  1. Open the main map view — the dashboard loads with 2025 baseline data already rendered.
  2. Select a scenario using the buttons in the left sidebar to compare 2025, 2050, 2075, and the greening policy.
  3. Click any neighbourhood to see its name, exact risk score (0–100), and colour classification.
  4. Focus on red and dark-red areas — these are your priority zones for intervention planning.
  5. Compare 2050 vs. 2050 + Greening to quantify the potential impact of a specific policy investment.
  6. Export or screenshot specific views to share with local decision-makers or include in reports.
09

Important Caveats

  • C1
    Neighbourhood averages hide internal variation. Within each boundary, pockets of extreme vulnerability exist. Block-by-block risk can be far higher than the neighbourhood score suggests.
  • C2
    We assume no behavioural adaptation. In reality, residents will buy air conditioning, relocate, or build mutual support networks. Our projections are deliberately conservative.
  • C3
    Health data gaps exist. 11,554 of 14,668 neighbourhoods have complete health data; the remainder are estimated. Edge areas may be less reliable.
  • C4
    Climate projections carry inherent uncertainty. +1.5°C and +3.0°C are scenario midpoints, not certainties. Real outcomes depend on global emissions decisions made today.
  • C5
    This is one model among many. Use alongside other tools, local knowledge, and community input. The best interventions combine data with ground-truth expertise.
  • C6
    Social factors remain partially unmeasured. Community cohesion, access to services, migration patterns, and informal support networks are not directly captured in any of the three datasets.
